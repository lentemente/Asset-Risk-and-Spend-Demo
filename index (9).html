Network asset risk · investment demonstrator

Spend by consequence, not just condition.

Replacing the most assets is not the same as removing the riskiest failures. With real unit costs, an end-of-life programme renews a lot of cheap kit; weighting the same budget by the safety and reliability consequence of failure clears the hot corner instead.

How it works

An illustrative fleet of about 62,100 assets across seven volumetric types is generated once and held fixed, with a realistic spread of ages: distribution transformers, 11 kV and 33 kV overhead lines and underground cables (per kilometre), support structures, and switchgear. Failure follows a Weibull hazard (shape 3) whose characteristic life is each fleet's expected life, scaled so the year-0 expected failures match a target (about 185 in total). Each asset carries a fixed safety and reliability impact grade and a zone (1–5); 33 kV assets sit mostly in the higher reliability zones. Matrix cells are grouped into three risk bands by impact and zone, and the figures are expected failures per year.

Replacement uses real unit rates: transformers $30k, switchgear $35k, overhead lines $100k/km, cables $1M/km, support structures $10k. The base spend is set from fleet lives — funding renewal at about three-quarters of the steady-state rate lives imply (~18% of the fleet over ten years). The end-of-life method renews the most life-expired assets first; the risk-based method renews the highest risk-reduction-per-pound first. At the same budget the end-of-life method replaces far more (cheaper) assets, while risk-based concentrates spend where failures do most harm. Renewal volume is bounded by resource commitments: a committed minimum the network will deliver (about half the life-based rate) and a deliverability ceiling it cannot exceed (about three times it), reflecting crews, outages and supply chains. In Move the investment a slider per fleet sets the annual volume within those bounds, and the worst-risk assets in each fleet are renewed first.

Every failure also carries a reactive repair cost — cheap for overhead lines (~$2.5k), modest for support structures (~$8k), and expensive for cables (~$60k), transformers and switchgear (~$30k). The views report this alongside the proactive investment as an all-in cost, which surfaces a real tension: the end-of-life programme trims repair spend by renewing expensive-to-fix kit, while risk-based concentrates on high-consequence overhead that is cheap to repair but dangerous to leave. Conductor volumes are set to 1,800 km of 11 kV and 300 km of 33 kV, each split between overhead and underground.

The long-term view projects the chosen per-fleet volumes 20 years forward, ageing the fleet one year at a time and renewing the committed volume of worst-risk assets each year (failed assets are assumed partly replaced in kind). It charts renewal spend, reactive repair spend and failures per year. Because risk-based targeting delivers a low volume of assets, deferring the bulk of the ageing fleet, sustained under-delivery builds a bow wave: failures and repair spend climb year on year, shown against the sustainable replacement rate. The numbers are synthetic; this is a demonstration of the method, not a model of any real network.
