Age-based vs risk-based — same budget, same fleet

Both programmes spend the same annual budget (set above) every year for 50 years, on the same fleet. Age-based renews whatever is most life-expired first; risk-based renews whatever failure does the most safety and reliability harm per dollar. Holding each fixed for 50 years is a deliberate stress test — the gap between them is the long-term cost of allocating by age instead of consequence.

Where each programme puts the money

The same budget, allocated two ways. Age-based pours into the oldest fleets (poles); risk-based fully funds cheap, high-consequence fleets like crossarms and switchgear that age-based leaves at zero — which is why their failures diverge by year 25.

How it works

An illustrative fleet of about 14,750 units across fourteen fleets is built once from a fixed profile (poles, crossarms, overhead conductor and underground cable per km, distribution transformers and switchgear). Each fleet ages on its own Weibull failure curve. Every view runs the same annual cycle — age the fleet a year, count the failures that fall out, repair or replace them, then renew the oldest assets up to your budget — so year 1 is identical no matter what you choose, and the panels can never disagree.

Each failure carries a safety and a reliability impact grade on a 0–3 scale — for safety, 0 = no injury potential, 1 = cuts and scrapes, 2 = minor burns or bruising, 3 = serious injury (electric shock, severe burns, or objects falling from height); for reliability, 0 = no loss of supply, 1 = a few LV customers, 2 = a section isolated for repair, 3 = protection trips and a widespread outage. Each failure also sits in a zone band (1–5) for how exposed that part of the network is. A cell’s risk is impact × zone (0–15), banded Low (≤2) / Moderate (3–5) / High (6–9) / Critical (≥10). The budget is allocated by consequence per dollar — the most harm retired per pound spent — which is why the spend concentrates on high-consequence poles, crossarms and switchgear rather than on whatever simply fails most often.

The two top controls are inverses of one another: drag investment to read off the long-term failure level it buys, or drag long-term failures to read off the budget required to hold it. Renewal is capped at each fleet’s sustainable replacement rate (about $5.6M/yr for the whole fleet); even at that ceiling some failures remain, because a fleet renewed exactly at its replacement rate still fails at a steady background rate. The numbers are synthetic and illustrative — this demonstrates the method, not any real network.
