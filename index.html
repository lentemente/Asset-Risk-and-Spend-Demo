Network asset risk · investment demonstrator

Spend by consequence, not just condition.

A failure is not just a failure. Each one lands somewhere on a safety and a reliability risk matrix, and the assets that fail most are rarely the ones that hurt most. This tool sizes where harm actually concentrates across a real-shaped fleet, and lets you move the renewal money to see the risk — and the long-term bow wave — respond.

Illustrative profile: 14 fleets, ~14,750 units. The subject is the method, not a real network.

How it works

An illustrative fleet of about 14,750 units across fourteen fleets is built once from a fixed profile: support structures (wood and concrete poles, crossarms), 11 kV and 33 kV overhead conductor and underground cable (per km), distribution transformers (ground- and pole-mounted) and switchgear (reclosers, air-break and gas switches). Each fleet has its own Weibull shape (steeper for structures, flatter for cable and conductor) and a characteristic life equal to its expected life, so failure emerges from the age profile rather than a fixed target. Each fleet's average age sets its starting age spread — fleets sitting well past mid-life read as behind on renewal.

Every failure carries a safety and a reliability impact grade (1–5) and sits in a zone band (1–5) describing how exposed or how many customers that part of the network serves. A cell's risk is impact × zone, banded Low (≤4), Moderate (5–9), High (10–14) and Critical (≥15), so the focus is the whole top-right region, not one corner. The matrix figures are expected failures per year.

Costs are per unit from the profile: a replacement rate for proactive renewal, and a repair rate for dealing with a failure. Each fleet also has an action upon failure: Replace fleets (e.g. poles) are renewed when they fail, so they self-limit; Repair fleets (e.g. cable, conductor, switchgear) are patched and keep ageing, so deferring their renewal lets reactive cost and failures compound. In Condition vs risk the same annual budget — the cost of the default programme — is spent two ways: most life-expired first, versus highest safety-and-reliability consequence first.

In Move the investment a slider per fleet sets annual volume between the committed minimum and the deliverable maximum from the profile, and the oldest assets in each fleet go first. The long-term view projects that programme 50 years forward, ageing the fleet a year at a time, renewing your committed volume of oldest assets and repairing or replacing whatever fails. It charts renewal and reactive spend, and failures per year — as a total against the sustainable replacement rate, or split by safety or reliability risk band. The numbers are synthetic; this demonstrates the method, not any real network.
